Golden Rules

5 Rules That Keep You Safe

Remember these simple rules. They'll protect you from almost every scam.

01

Never share personal information

No legitimate company will call, text, or email asking for your Social Insurance Number, bank details, or passwords.

02

Hang up and call back yourself

If someone claims to be your bank or the government, hang up and dial the number on your card or their official website.

03

Don't click unexpected links

If a text or email has a link you weren't expecting, don't tap it. Ask someone you trust first.

04

It's okay to say "no"

You're never obligated to give money or information on the spot. A real organization will always understand.

05

Tell someone you trust

If something feels off, talk to a family member, a friend, or call one of the helplines on our Resources page.

Spot a Scam

Real examples of the calls, texts, and emails scammers actually send — and exactly what gives them away.

Scam Example

"This is the CRA. You owe $5,000 in back taxes. Pay with gift cards in the next hour or a warrant will be issued for your arrest."

Reveal the Red Flags
  • Demands payment by gift card
  • Threatens arrest over the phone
  • Creates urgency with a tight deadline
What to doHang up. The CRA never threatens arrest or asks for gift cards. Call the CRA directly using the number on cra-arc.gc.ca if you're unsure.
Scam Example

"Hi Grandma, it's me! I'm in trouble and need you to e-transfer $2,000 right away. Please don't tell Mom and Dad."

Reveal the Red Flags
  • Vague identity — relies on you to fill in the blank
  • Urgent request to keep it secret
  • Asks for money before you can verify who's calling
What to doHang up and call that grandchild, or another family member, on a number you already have saved.
Scam Example

"We've detected a virus on your computer. Stay on the line while we connect you to remote support to fix it for you."

Reveal the Red Flags
  • Unsolicited call about a problem you never reported
  • Asks for remote access to your computer
What to doHang up. Real tech companies don't call you first, and a stranger should never get remote access to your computer.
Scam Example

"Canada Post: Your package could not be delivered. Confirm your address and pay a $1.99 redelivery fee: canadapost-redeliver.fake-link.com"

Reveal the Red Flags
  • Link doesn't match the real Canada Post website
  • Small "fee" requested through a text link
  • An unexpected delivery notice
What to doDon't tap the link. Check tracking directly on canadapost.ca if you're expecting something.
Scam Example

"ALERT: Your bank account has been compromised. Tap here immediately to verify your identity or your account will be frozen."

Reveal the Red Flags
  • Urgency combined with a threat
  • Asks you to click a link to "verify" your identity
What to doDon't tap it. Open your banking app directly, or call the number on your card instead.
Scam Example

"Congratulations! You've won a $1,000 gift card. Claim it now: bit.ly/claim-prize99"

Reveal the Red Flags
  • A prize you never entered to win
  • A shortened link hiding the real destination
What to doDelete the message. A real prize never requires you to click a link to "claim" it.
Scam Example

"From: support@amaz0n-secure.com — Subject: Your account has been locked. Dear valued costumer, we detected unusuall activity. Click below to verify your identity."

Reveal the Red Flags
  • Misspelled sender domain ("amaz0n" with a zero)
  • Spelling mistakes — "costumer," "unusuall"
  • Generic greeting and urgent tone
What to doDon't click anything. Go to amazon.ca directly in your browser to check your account.
Scam Example

"You have been named beneficiary to a $4.5 million inheritance from a distant relative. Send a $500 processing fee and your bank details to claim it."

Reveal the Red Flags
  • An unexpected windfall from someone you've never heard of
  • An upfront "fee" requested
  • Asks for your banking details
What to doDelete the email. No legitimate inheritance ever requires you to pay a fee first.
Scam Example

"CRA Notice: You are eligible for a refund of $375.50. Click here to submit your claim within 24 hours."

Reveal the Red Flags
  • A tight 24-hour deadline
  • Asks you to click a link to "claim" money
  • The CRA doesn't email refund links
What to doDon't click it. Log in to your CRA My Account directly, or call the CRA to check.

Device Help

Plain-language answers for your phone, computer, and the internet. Tap any question to see the answer.

Using Your Smartphone

How do I turn my phone on and off?

Press and hold the power button (usually on the side or top edge) for a few seconds. A menu will appear — slide or tap "Power Off." To turn it back on, hold the same button until the screen lights up.

How do I make the text bigger?

Go to Settings, then look for "Display" or "Accessibility." You'll find a text size slider there — drag it to the right to make everything easier to read.

How do I send a text message?

Open the Messages app, tap the icon to start a new message, choose a contact, type your message in the box at the bottom, and tap the arrow or "Send" button.

How do I take a screenshot?

On most phones, press the power button and the volume-down button at the same time for one second. You'll hear a click or see a flash — the image is saved to your Photos app.

Computer Basics

How do I use the internet browser?

Open the browser icon (it often looks like a globe or a colorful circle). Click the bar at the top, type the website name or what you're looking for, and press Enter.

How do I copy and paste text?

Highlight the text by clicking and dragging over it, then press Ctrl+C (or Cmd+C on a Mac) to copy. Click where you want it to go, then press Ctrl+V (or Cmd+V) to paste.

How do I shut down my computer safely?

Click the Start menu (Windows) or Apple menu (Mac), then choose "Shut Down." Always use this option instead of simply unplugging the computer.

WiFi & Internet

How do I connect to WiFi?

Go to Settings and tap "WiFi." Choose your home network from the list, enter the password if asked, and tap "Connect." You'll usually only need to do this once.

How do I know if a website is safe?

Look for "https://" and a small padlock icon next to the website address. Be cautious of sites with strange spelling in the name or that ask for personal information right away.

Video Calling

How do I make a video call with FaceTime?

On an iPhone or iPad, open FaceTime, tap the "+" button, choose a contact, and tap the video camera icon to start the call.

How do I make a video call with Zoom or WhatsApp?

Open the app, find the contact, and tap the video camera icon. If someone sends you a meeting link, just tap it — the app will open the call automatically.

Email Made Easy

How do I send an email with a photo attached?

Start a new email, then look for a paperclip icon. Tap it, choose the photo from your gallery, and it will attach to your message before you hit send.

How do I tell if an email is spam?

Watch for urgent language, spelling mistakes, an unfamiliar sender address, or requests for personal information. When in doubt, don't click any links — delete it instead.

Passwords & Security

How do I create a strong password?

Use at least 12 characters with a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols. A string of random words you'll remember, like "PurpleCoffee7Garden!", is both strong and easy to recall.

Should I write my passwords down?

A written list kept somewhere private and secure — not next to your computer — is far better than reusing the same password everywhere. Even better, ask a family member about password manager apps.

What is two-factor authentication?

It's a second check, like a code texted to your phone, that confirms it's really you logging in. Turn it on wherever it's offered — it's one of the strongest protections available.

The Red-Flag Checklist

Print this and keep it by the phone. Any single checkmark is reason enough to stop, hang up, and check with someone you trust.

  • They contacted me first
  • They want me to act immediately, or keep it secret
  • They're asking for gift cards, wire transfer, e-transfer, or cryptocurrency
  • They want a password, SIN, or banking information
  • They want remote access to my computer
  • Something just feels off
